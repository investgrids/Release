Product Review — Internal EventIQ v0.2.0 · 2026-07-03 · Head of Product
Overall Verdict

EventIQ is a product that tried to become Bloomberg in six months and ended up being nothing in particular. It has 25 pages, a single genuine differentiator, and the differentiator is sidebar item number four.

The Ripple Engine is a legitimately original idea with real technical execution behind it. Everything else on this product is commodity — and commodity built worse than the free alternatives your target users already use every morning.

Cut twelve pages. Fix one critical bug that makes every event show "Low Impact." Make the Ripple Engine the product, not a feature. Then ship.

Section 1

The Identity Crisis

EventIQ does not know what it is. This is the root cause of every other problem.

Count what the product is trying to be simultaneously: a market data terminal, a news aggregator, a stock screener, a company comparison tool, an AI investment advisor, an event intelligence platform, and an economic calendar. That is seven products competing for the same screen real estate, the same user attention, and the same engineering roadmap.

The result is that it does none of these things well enough to displace what users already have. Moneycontrol has better news. Zerodha Kite has better market data. Screener.in has better stock financials. NSE India has a real economic calendar. Each of these incumbents has a decade of iteration and a dedicated user base.

There is exactly one thing on this product that none of those incumbents have: the Ripple Engine. An AI-generated causal dependency graph that shows how one event cascades through 4 levels of economic consequence — sectors, companies, commodities, currencies, policy responses — with confidence scoring on every edge.

The product should be: "We show you what happens next when a market event hits — in a way nobody else can." That is the entire brief. Everything that doesn't serve that sentence should be cut or deprioritised until further notice.

Instead, the Ripple Engine is buried behind Dashboard, Market Intelligence, and Events in the sidebar. A first-time user looking at the navigation has no idea this product has something the others don't.

Section 2

Navigation: Structurally Broken

The sidebar has 21 items. The header has 8 different items. They do not match. This is not a design problem — it is a signal that nobody owns navigation decisions.

The header and sidebar are two different products

Header navigation: Dashboard, Events, Stories, News, Radar, Explorer, Compare, Calendar.

Sidebar navigation: Dashboard, Market Intelligence, Events, Ripple Engine, Stories, News, Opportunity Radar, Companies, Compare Companies, Economic Calendar, IPO Hub, Market Indices, Sectors, Heatmap, Market Breadth, Commodities & Energy, AI Search, Market Signals, AI Market Wrap, Top Themes, Government Policies.

A user arriving on any page not in the header nav will find the header useless for navigation. A user arriving on the homepage will not find Ripple Engine in the header at all — the product's single differentiator is not in the top navigation. This is a fundamental information architecture failure.

The taxonomy is fake

The sidebar groups are "Primary," "Market Overview," and "Intelligence." These categories encode how the engineering team thinks about features, not how users think about their jobs. No user opens a market intelligence platform thinking "I need to check the Market Overview section." They think "what happened overnight" or "show me how this RBI decision affects my portfolio."

Navigation should be organised around user tasks, not feature categories. The current structure organises around what the product has, not what the user does.

21 items is not navigation — it is a sitemap

At 21 sidebar items, every page dilutes every other page. When everything is a top-level item, nothing is important. Bloomberg Terminal has thousands of functions, but it organises them through a command-line interface and function keys — not 21 equally-weighted sidebar links. For a consumer web product, the cognitive budget for navigation is roughly 5 to 7 items before users stop processing the list and start ignoring it.

Section 3

Pages That Should Not Exist

Eleven of the current 25 pages have no business being standalone routes. They are tabs, widgets, or redundant surfaces that inflate the navigation without adding user value.

  • KILL
    /market-breadth — A standalone page for one metric, currently displaying hardcoded values (1124 advances, 387 declines, 89 unchanged). These numbers never change. If users ever notice, the product is dead to them. This should be a widget inside a Markets page.
  • KILL
    /heatmap — A treemap view of sector performance. This is a visualisation mode, not a destination. It belongs as a toggle inside Markets alongside a table view and a chart view. There is no user who bookmarks a heatmap standalone page.
  • KILL
    /market-indices — 12 index cards with mini area charts. Fully subsumed by the Market Intelligence page, which already has an indices tab. Duplicate surface, duplicate maintenance cost.
  • KILL
    /sectors — Sector performance cards, also available inside Market Intelligence. Another standalone page for data that already lives inside a tab elsewhere.
  • KILL
    /markets — The "Commodities & Energy" sidebar item routes here. Four commodity prices (Brent, Gold, DXY, USD/INR). This is a widget, possibly a section inside a Pre-Market panel. It is not a page.
  • KILL
    /themes — Investment themes (Defence, EV, AI, Green Energy). Identical purpose to Stories. These two pages exist because someone added a "Themes" page after building "Stories" without checking what already existed. One of them has to go; probably both should be absorbed into Opportunity Radar.
  • KILL
    /stories — Editorial narrative cards about market themes. Indistinguishable from Themes. The distinction between a "story" and a "theme" is not legible to any user. This is a content taxonomy that makes sense internally and means nothing externally.
  • KILL
    /ai-wrap — "AI Market Wrap." An end-of-day AI summary. The Dashboard already has an AI market summary. Market Signals does the same. This is the third surface for AI-generated market commentary. Pick one, make it excellent, cut the other two.
  • KILL
    /market-signals — AI-derived market signals. Duplicate purpose with the AI Market Wrap and the Dashboard AI summary. Three AI commentary surfaces with no differentiation between them is not a feature set — it is a product team that never said no to a feature request.
  • KILL
    /policies — Government Policies from PIB RSS feeds. PIB press releases are often in Hindi, are not formatted for market context, and have no integration with the events they relate to in the UI. This page has no discernible user workflow attached to it. If the policy data matters, integrate it into Event Detail as a related policies section. Do not give it a standalone page.
  • KILL
    /search — A search page alongside a separate /ai-search page. Two search entry points with overlapping or identical function. The global search in the header already routes to /ai-search. Pick one search surface and commit to it. There is no user mental model in which a product has two different search pages.
Section 4

The Dashboard Is a Dumping Ground

The dashboard has twelve named section components. No dashboard should have twelve named sections. This is what happens when every feature team demands homepage real estate and nobody says no.

Count them: SmartHero, PreMarketPanel, MarketOverviewSection, SectorPerformanceCard, TopEventsSection, AIOpportunitySection, TopMoversGrid, DashboardBottomRow, DashboardRightSidebar, EconomicCalendar, LatestNews, FloatingAISearch. A three-column layout at 1600px wide trying to fit the entire product on one page.

A dashboard has one job: show the user the most important thing happening right now and make the next action obvious. That is not what this dashboard does. It shows everything simultaneously, which is functionally the same as showing nothing in particular. Users on a good dashboard should be able to answer in three seconds: "What happened overnight that I need to know about?" Currently, they would have to scan through twelve sections to find an answer that should be on the first visible screen.

The right dashboard for this product is: the top 3 highest-impact events from the last 24 hours, their Ripple graphs accessible in one click, and a pre-market summary. That is the morning briefing. Everything else is secondary.

The 3-column layout also fails at a more basic level: the rightmost column (260px) is too narrow to contain anything substantive, so it becomes a dumping ground for supplementary widgets that compete for attention with the primary content. This is a layout that emerged from "we have three columns in the grid" rather than from what information actually needs to exist at each viewport position.

Section 5

Broken Trust Signals

A financial product lives and dies on data trust. EventIQ has at least five features that will actively destroy user trust the moment they are noticed — and they will be noticed.

The impact score bug is live in production

The backend stores impact_score as a float between 0 and 10. The Events page compares that value against thresholds of 90, 75, and 55, which assume a 0–100 scale. An event with impact_score = 8.5 — which is extremely high — displays as "Low Impact" because 8.5 is less than 55. This means every single event in the product currently shows incorrect impact labels. The most important signal the product provides — event impact — is wrong for all data. This is not a minor bug. It is the primary thing the Events page communicates, and it is broken.

Hardcoded market breadth that never changes

The Market Breadth page shows 1124 advances, 387 declines, and 89 unchanged. These numbers are hardcoded in the component. They do not change between sessions, between days, or between market conditions. A user who visits this page twice in the same week will see the same numbers. In financial data products, static numbers that claim to be live data are one of the fastest ways to lose a user permanently.

The "Live" badge that lies

The News page shows a green pulsing dot with "Live · refreshes every 15 min." The page does not auto-refresh. The component mounts, calls the API once, and never calls it again. There is no interval, no polling, no WebSocket. This badge is a cosmetic label added to look real-time — it is not backed by any behavior. Users on a news page notice when headlines do not update. When they do, they will not come back.

Hardcoded AI Signal Strength

The Opportunity Radar sidebar shows "Bullish 72% / Neutral 18% / Bearish 10%." These values are hardcoded in the component. They have no connection to the actual opportunity data, the actual market state, or any AI model output. They are decorative numbers that simulate intelligence without providing it. Remove them entirely or compute them from actual data — there is no acceptable middle ground.

Stubs presented as features

The "Add Alert" button on the Events page does nothing. The bookmark icon does nothing. The share buttons do nothing. The "Calendar View" toggle does nothing. The "Pro Upgrade" card in the sidebar links nowhere. The "More Filters" button on Radar does nothing.

In a product demo, non-functional UI elements feel like a roadmap. In a live product, they feel like abandonment. Every non-functional element on a live product costs user trust. The rule is simple: if it does nothing, do not render it.

Section 6

The Ripple Engine: Right Idea, Wrong Position

The Ripple Engine is the only thing on this product that could make a sophisticated user say "I can't get this anywhere else." That makes it the most valuable asset in the entire codebase. It is also positioned, discovered, and presented as if it were a secondary feature.

Discovery is broken

It is sidebar item number four. The user has to scroll past Dashboard, Market Intelligence, and Events to find it. It does not appear in the header navigation at all. The product's most defensible feature is not reachable from the main navigation bar that persists on every page.

The hub page is a marketing page, not a product

The Ripple hub at /ripple contains a hero section explaining what the Ripple Engine is, a "How it works" grid, and a list of event cards to click. There is no scenario input field. There is no company search. There is no theme selector. The only way to enter the Ripple Engine is by clicking one of the pre-selected event cards — which means if a user wants to run a Ripple analysis on an event or scenario that isn't in the grid, they have no path to do it from the hub.

The company and theme Ripple endpoints exist in the backend — GET /api/ripple/company/{ticker} and GET /api/ripple/theme/{theme_slug} are both implemented. There is no UI to reach them. The APIs are built and the front door doesn't exist.

Generation has no feedback loop

When a Ripple graph is not cached, generation takes 5 to 15 seconds. There is no streaming progress, no estimated time, no partial render. The user clicks an event card and waits with no feedback. At 10 seconds, most users will assume the product is broken and leave. This is a solvable UX problem with no code change — a generation progress indicator and a clear "building your graph…" state would recover most of that drop-off.

After the graph, there is nothing

A user views a Ripple graph. They can explore the nodes, read the Insights tab, check the node legend. Then what? There is no "explore this company" CTA from a company node. There is no "run a what-if scenario from this point" flow. There is no share button, no export, no permalink. After the graph, the product ends. The most engaging moment in the entire product has no exit that keeps the user in the product.

Section 7

News: A Page With No Defensible Advantage

The News page aggregates RSS feeds from the Economic Times, Business Standard, LiveMint, Reuters, and Moneycontrol. These sources already have their own websites, their own apps, and their own mobile notifications. Why would a user read their content here?

There is only one defensible reason to have a news page in this product: because news is connected to events, and events are what EventIQ understands. If clicking a news article showed which market events it relates to, what sectors it touches, and what the Ripple cascade looks like — that would be a differentiated news experience. Currently, the news page is a feed of summaries with gradient placeholder thumbnails and a tab bar that does keyword classification.

The category tabs (Market / Economy / Corporate / Policy / Global) use client-side keyword matching. The word "nifty" assigns an article to Market. The word "gdp" assigns it to Economy. The word "merger" assigns it to Corporate. This approach is both inaccurate and unmaintainable. An article about the "GDP impact on nifty" would match both Economy and Market — the first match wins, and the assignment is effectively arbitrary for many articles.

The company linking in news articles uses a 30-entry hardcoded dictionary that maps company names to NSE symbols. "Reliance Industries" maps to RELIANCE. "Tata Consultancy" maps to TCS. Any company not in this dictionary — including every mid-cap and small-cap stock — gets no link. This is not a feature; it is a feature-shaped approximation.

The News page should either be rebuilt around the product's core concept (events-linked, Ripple-aware news) or it should be cut. As it stands, it is a weaker version of what users can get from Google News with a finance filter.

Section 8

Market Data: Commodity All the Way Down

Market Intelligence, Market Indices, Sectors, Heatmap, Market Breadth, and Commodities are six surfaces serving the same user need — market data overview — with 15-minute delayed yfinance data. This is the most crowded category in Indian fintech and the one where EventIQ has the least advantage.

Moneycontrol, NSE India, BSE India, Economic Times Markets, Zerodha Varsity, TradingView — all of these provide market data faster, with more depth, with real-time pricing, and with years of user trust. The argument for building a market data section in EventIQ is not that EventIQ will have better market data — it is that market data provides context for events. That context argument is only valid if the market data is actually connected to event analysis. Currently, the market data pages exist in isolation from the events.

The pre-market section is the strongest part of the market data offering — the AI Opening Prediction (bull%/bear%/neutral% with reasoning) is a genuine differentiator. The rest — index cards, sector bars, a heatmap — is table stakes that adds engineering burden without adding user value beyond what incumbents offer for free.

Six standalone pages for market data is also a navigation disaster that dilutes the sidebar and makes the product feel wider than it is deep. All of this should be one tabbed "Markets" page, and the pre-market section with the AI prediction should be the hero tab.

Section 9

Companies: The Hardcoded Ceiling

The Companies page — one of the more capable sections in the product — limits itself by design to 18 hardcoded stocks. Adding a 19th requires a code deployment.

On page load, the browser makes 18 simultaneous API calls to fetch prices for 18 predetermined stocks. This is an architectural choice that announces itself: the company list was small enough that batching didn't seem necessary. At 50 stocks it becomes a performance problem. At 100 it becomes untenable. There is no reason to not implement a single /api/stocks/batch?symbols=... endpoint, but nobody has prioritised it because the list is hardcoded at 18 and seems to work.

The Trending chips below the search bar — BEL, HAL, RVNL, RELIANCE, HDFCBANK, LT, NTPC, TATAMOTORS, INFY, TCS — are also hardcoded. They are not trending based on any data. They are not the most-searched symbols. They are not event-correlated. They are a list someone typed in once. This is the wrong kind of editorial voice for a data product. Either derive trending symbols from actual query data, or remove the "Trending" label and call it "Popular."

The Compare Companies feature is genuinely useful and technically well-built. It allows comparison of up to 4 stocks across 12 tabs including financials, valuation, performance, and cash flow. It is sidebar item number nine — behind Dashboard, Market Intelligence, Events, Ripple Engine, Stories, News, Opportunity Radar, and Companies. The one feature in the companies section that provides unique value is the hardest one to find.

AI capabilities that are missing

The product calls itself "event-driven market intelligence" but the AI doesn't do the most obvious event-intelligence tasks:

  • MISSING
    Historical precedent — "When India's repo rate was cut by 50bps in 2019, banking stocks moved X% over 30 days." The product has event data and stock data but no capability to connect them historically. This would make Ripple predictions verifiable and trusted.
  • MISSING
    Conversational drill-down on Ripple graphs — After viewing a graph, users should be able to ask "why does this event affect Aviation?" and get an explanation. The graph is currently view-only. There is no way to interrogate a specific edge or node.
  • MISSING
    Event-to-news linking via NLP — Articles are ingested as summaries. No entity extraction, no event linkage, no automatic tagging. A news article about RBI policy is not connected to RBI events in any automated way. This connection exists in the data model (event_news table) but is never populated.
  • MISSING
    Impact prediction with price targets — Ripple shows qualitative cascade effects but gives no quantitative estimate. "HDFCBANK: estimated -2% to -4% NIM compression over 2 quarters" would transform the graph from an explanatory tool to a decision-support tool.
  • MISSING
    Alert intelligence — "Alert me when any event with confidence > 80% touches the Banking sector." The alerts infrastructure exists but the trigger logic doesn't. Currently alerts are manual-only.
  • MISSING
    Opportunity validation — The Radar generates AI investment opportunities but has no mechanism to track whether predictions were accurate. Without a feedback loop, the Radar is an opinion generator, not an intelligence product.
Section 11

Scalability: Walls at Every Level

The current architecture hits hard walls at several points before any meaningful scale. None of these are obscure edge cases — they are the first things that would happen if the product got real users.

  • AUTH
    No authentication — means no personalisation ever. Watchlists, saved Ripple graphs, alert subscriptions, personalised dashboards — none of these are possible without auth. The product ceiling is "public terminal." This is not a feature gap; it is a structural decision that caps the product's ceiling.
  • DATA
    No Alembic migrations — the schema is created with create_all() on startup. The first time a production schema change is needed — adding a column, changing a type — the options are: drop and recreate the database (losing all data), or write raw SQL migration scripts by hand. Neither is acceptable. This needs to be addressed before any production deployment.
  • INFRA
    APScheduler in-process — runs inside the same uvicorn worker. If the process crashes, the scheduler restarts with no job state. At multiple workers, every worker runs its own scheduler, causing duplicate job execution. This is fine for a single-server dev setup. It does not survive horizontal scaling.
  • AI
    Free-tier LLMs with no rate limiting — Ripple generation calls free-tier models (Groq, Cerebras, Gemini, OpenRouter). There is no rate limiting on the Ripple endpoints. A moderately popular article linking to the product could exhaust the daily free quota for all providers simultaneously. There is no queuing, no request coalescing, no graceful degradation beyond the 5-template fallback system.
  • API
    18 parallel stock API calls per page load — The Companies page fetches all 18 stocks simultaneously on mount. At 50 concurrent users, that is 900 simultaneous yfinance requests. yfinance has no official API agreement and will throttle or block at any meaningful load.
  • STATE
    In-memory dict cache not shared across processes — The market data TTL cache is a Python dict in each worker process. In a multi-worker deployment, each worker maintains its own cache independently, defeating the caching benefit and multiplying upstream API calls by the number of workers.
  • DATA
    yfinance 15-minute delay — Every market data surface uses yfinance, which provides delayed data. Calling it "Live" — as the News page does — or presenting it alongside live-looking ticker animations creates a false impression of data freshness. At any traffic level that matters, this will be noticed and criticized.

Summary

What to Do First

Not everything above can be fixed at once. This is the sequence that matters.

  • TODAY
    Fix the impact score bug — Change the frontend threshold comparisons from 90/75/55 to 9/7.5/5.5 to match the backend's 0–10 scale. This takes 5 minutes and fixes every event impact label in the product. It should already be fixed. Every day it is not, the product's primary information is wrong.
  • TODAY
    Remove all non-functional UI elements — "Add Alert," "Bookmark," "Share," "Calendar View," "More Filters," "Pro Upgrade." Remove every button, icon, and label that does nothing. They are not features in progress — they are broken promises.
  • WEEK 1
    Move Ripple Engine to sidebar position 1 — It should be the first item. Rename it to something a non-technical user understands: "Impact Analysis" or "Market Cascade" or simply "Ripple." Add a scenario input and company/theme search to the hub page. Add company and theme entry points using the backend endpoints that already exist.
  • WEEK 1
    Kill the 11 unnecessary pages — Remove /market-breadth, /heatmap, /market-indices, /sectors, /markets, /stories, /themes, /ai-wrap, /market-signals, /policies, /search from the navigation. Consolidate into the 6-item structure above. The product will be smaller and clearer.
  • WEEK 2
    Set up Alembic before any further schema changes — One schema change without migrations in production will destroy user data. This is not optional.
  • MONTH 1
    Add progress feedback to Ripple generation — A "building your ripple graph…" state with a progress indicator covering the 5–15 second AI generation window. This will materially improve the retention rate at the product's most important user moment.
  • MONTH 1
    Connect news to events — The event_news junction table exists. Populate it. When a user reads a news article, show which events it relates to. When a user views an event, show the news articles about it. This is the only thing that makes a news page defensible in this product.
  • MONTH 2
    Authentication — Without it, the product cannot remember anything about its users, cannot personalise, cannot send alerts, and cannot differentiate free from paid. Every feature built after this point without auth is built on a ceiling.

The product that should exist is this: you open it in the morning, it shows you three events that matter today, you click one, and in ten seconds you see exactly how that event ripples through every sector, company, and indicator in the Indian market. That product does not exist yet. Everything else on the roadmap is secondary to building that experience — cleanly, correctly, and completely.
